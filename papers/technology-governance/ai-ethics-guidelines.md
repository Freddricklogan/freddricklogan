# AI Ethics in Educational Technology

## Bias Auditing

Every AI system deployed in a student-facing context should undergo bias auditing before deployment and at regular intervals thereafter. This means testing model outputs across demographic groups (race, gender, income, first-generation status) to identify disparate impact. Require vendors to provide documentation of their training data composition, validation methodology, and fairness metrics. If a vendor cannot or will not provide this information, that is a disqualifying factor. No institution should deploy an AI system that makes consequential student decisions without understanding how it performs across its full population.

## Transparency Standards

Students have a right to know when AI is influencing decisions about their education. Establish institutional standards requiring disclosure whenever automated systems are used for admissions screening, early alert identification, course recommendations, financial aid determinations, or academic integrity detection. Disclosures should be clear and accessible, not buried in privacy policies. Explain what the system does, what data it uses, and how students can raise concerns or request human review.

## Human-in-the-Loop Requirements

AI should augment human judgment, not replace it. For any decision that materially affects a student's academic standing, financial aid, or enrollment status, require human review before action is taken. An early alert system can flag a student for potential intervention, but the decision to intervene -- and how -- should involve an advisor who understands the student's full context. Automated escalation without human oversight creates a system where students are treated as data points rather than people.

## Institutional Review Process

Establish an AI Review Committee modeled on institutional review boards. Before any AI tool is deployed in a student-facing context, it should undergo review for ethical implications, bias risk, transparency requirements, and alignment with institutional values. The committee should include technologists, faculty, student representatives, and ethics experts. Review should be ongoing, not one-time -- AI systems change over time as models are retrained and vendor priorities shift.
