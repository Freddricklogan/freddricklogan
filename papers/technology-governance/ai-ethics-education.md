# AI Ethics in Educational Technology

The rapid adoption of AI tools in education raises ethical questions that institutions are only beginning to address. Adaptive learning platforms personalize content based on student behavior. Predictive analytics identify at-risk students before they fail. Automated systems screen applications, flag potential plagiarism, and recommend courses. Each of these applications carries the potential for both significant benefit and significant harm, depending on how they are designed, deployed, and governed.

The core ethical tension is between efficiency and equity. AI systems trained on historical data will reproduce historical patterns -- including patterns of inequity. A retention model trained on data from an institution that has historically underserved certain populations will flag students from those populations as "at risk" at higher rates, potentially channeling them into interventions that are patronizing rather than supportive. Without deliberate attention to fairness, AI in education risks becoming a mechanism for automating existing biases at scale.

Governance is the answer, but it has to be the right kind of governance. Blanket bans on AI are impractical and counterproductive -- the tools are too useful to ignore, and students are already using them. What institutions need are clear policies that define acceptable uses, require transparency and human oversight, mandate bias testing, and establish review processes that keep pace with rapidly evolving technology. The institutions that get this right will be the ones that treat AI ethics not as a compliance exercise but as a core component of their educational mission.
