# Cybersecurity Governance for Educational Institutions

## Organizational Structure

Effective cybersecurity governance starts with clear accountability. The CISO should report to the CIO or, in larger institutions, directly to executive leadership through a risk management channel. A cross-functional Information Security Committee -- including representatives from academic affairs, student services, finance, and legal counsel -- should meet quarterly to review risk posture, approve policy changes, and allocate resources. Without distributed ownership, security becomes "IT's problem" and never gets the institutional attention it requires.

## Risk Assessment Methodology

Adopt a risk assessment methodology tailored to education's unique threat landscape: decentralized IT environments, BYOD culture, research data with varying sensitivity levels, and a population that turns over every semester. Conduct formal assessments annually using NIST 800-171 or ISO 27005, supplemented by targeted assessments whenever significant system changes occur. Document risk tolerance explicitly -- which risks the institution accepts, mitigates, or transfers -- and review those decisions with leadership annually.

## Policy Framework

Build a policy hierarchy with an overarching Information Security Policy supported by domain-specific policies: acceptable use, access control, incident response, data classification, vendor risk management, and business continuity. Each policy needs a designated owner, an annual review cycle, and clear enforcement mechanisms. Map controls to regulatory requirements (FERPA, GLBA, HIPAA where applicable, state breach laws) through a unified compliance matrix that reduces duplication and simplifies audits.

## Continuous Improvement

Governance is not a document you file and forget. Build continuous improvement through regular security metrics reporting, tabletop exercises, penetration testing, and post-incident reviews. Track metrics that matter: mean time to detect and respond to incidents, patch currency rates, phishing simulation results, and policy exception counts. Present these to leadership in a format that connects security performance to institutional risk.
