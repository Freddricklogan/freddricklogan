# Cybersecurity Governance for Educational Institutions

Educational institutions face a cybersecurity challenge that is structurally different from the private sector. The academic tradition of open access and collaboration runs directly counter to the security principle of restricting information flow. Universities host thousands of unmanaged devices, operate research networks with minimal restrictions, and serve a user base where 25% turns over annually through graduation and enrollment. This creates an attack surface that traditional corporate security models aren't designed to handle.

Governance for this environment must be both rigorous and realistic. Start with the NIST Cybersecurity Framework as a baseline -- it's flexible enough to accommodate education's decentralized culture while providing the structure needed for systematic risk management. Establish a security steering committee with representation beyond IT: include academic affairs, student services, research administration, and legal. This cross-functional approach ensures that security policies account for the institution's actual operations rather than an idealized version of them.

The most effective governance structures treat security as a risk management discipline rather than a compliance exercise. This means conducting honest risk assessments, documenting risk tolerance decisions explicitly, and accepting that some risks are worth taking in service of the academic mission. A completely locked-down network would be the most secure, but it would also be incompatible with education. The governance challenge is finding the right balance -- and being transparent about the tradeoffs.
