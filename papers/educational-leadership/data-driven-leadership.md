# Data-Driven Decision Making in Educational Administration

## Selecting the Right KPIs

The temptation with data-driven leadership is to measure everything. Resist it. Identify five to seven key performance indicators that directly connect to institutional strategic priorities. For student success, that might include first-to-second year retention by demographic, average time to degree, and course DFW rates in gateway courses. For operational efficiency, measure process completion times, manual intervention rates, and cost per student served. Each KPI should have a clear owner, a defined data source, and a target that's ambitious but achievable.

## Dashboard Design

A dashboard is only useful if it drives action. Design dashboards that surface insights, not just data. Use comparative views (this semester vs. last, this cohort vs. prior) and allow disaggregation by student population, program, and campus. Highlight exceptions and trends rather than displaying every metric equally. Place the most actionable information at the top. And test your dashboards with the people who will actually use them -- administrators who aren't data analysts need different presentations than institutional research staff.

## Building Analytical Capacity

Data-driven culture doesn't emerge from purchasing a BI platform. It requires building analytical literacy across leadership. Train administrators to distinguish correlation from causation, understand confidence intervals, and recognize when sample sizes are too small for conclusions. Create norms around evidence-based discussion in leadership meetings: present the data, discuss interpretations, and document the reasoning behind decisions. Leaders who acknowledge what the data doesn't show make better decisions than those who treat dashboards as truth.

## The Decision Cycle

Connect data to action through a structured cycle: identify a question, gather relevant data, analyze and interpret, design an intervention, implement, and measure results. Without this feedback loop, data collection is just overhead. Every report should end with "so what?" -- what does this data suggest we should do differently? If the answer is "nothing," the report may not be measuring what matters.
