# Cybersecurity Governance for Education

Education occupies an unusual position in the cybersecurity landscape. Unlike financial services or healthcare, where locking down systems is an accepted cost of doing business, educational institutions are fundamentally built on openness -- open networks, open inquiry, open access to information. A university network might host thousands of personal devices on any given day, run research labs that require unrestricted outbound traffic, and operate public-facing services from library catalogs to event registrations. Layer on FERPA obligations, limited IT budgets, and a user population that turns over by 25 percent every year, and you have a threat environment that generic corporate governance frameworks simply don't address.

The threat vectors hitting education are well-documented but worth restating because the specifics matter for governance. Ransomware attacks against school districts and universities have accelerated significantly, with attackers targeting institutions precisely because they know downtime during enrollment or finals is unacceptable -- making victims more likely to pay. Phishing remains the primary entry point, and the combination of .edu email addresses with high turnover means awareness training is a perpetual need, not an annual checkbox. Insider threats are a distinct concern too: student workers with database access, researchers with administrative privileges on lab systems, and departing employees whose accounts linger for months after separation.

Building a governance structure on a constrained budget means making hard choices about where to invest. Start with the basics: establish a security steering committee that includes representation from academic affairs, not just IT and administration. Adopt a recognized framework -- NIST Cybersecurity Framework works well for education because it's flexible and doesn't require expensive certification. Prioritize asset inventory and data classification, because you can't protect what you haven't cataloged. Implement role-based access controls tied to your HR and SIS systems. And budget for incident response planning before you need it. The institutions that weather breaches well are the ones that rehearsed their response, knew who to call, and had communication templates ready before the crisis hit.
