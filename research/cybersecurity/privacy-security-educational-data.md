# Privacy and Security in Educational Data

Student data protection operates under a regulatory framework that is simultaneously rigid and full of gray areas. FERPA establishes the baseline for higher education -- institutions must protect education records and cannot release personally identifiable information without consent, with specific exceptions for directory information and legitimate educational interest. For K-12, COPPA adds requirements around parental consent for children under thirteen. But the practical challenge isn't understanding these statutes in the abstract; it's applying them to a technology landscape where student data flows through dozens of systems, many operated by third-party vendors. When a faculty member requires students to use a commercial tool for coursework, that's a data-sharing arrangement whether anyone formally recognizes it or not.

Third-party vendor agreements deserve far more scrutiny than most institutions give them. Every EdTech platform that ingests student data -- from plagiarism detection services to adaptive learning tools to engagement analytics -- should be covered by a data processing agreement that specifies what data is collected, how it can be used, who owns it, and what happens when the contract ends. Institutions should maintain a vendor registry that catalogs every third-party tool touching student data, reviewed annually and tied to procurement so that new tools can't enter the ecosystem without a privacy review.

Practical data governance starts with classification. Not all student data carries the same risk. Directory information is different from academic records, which is different from sensitive personal data like disability accommodations, counseling records, or financial information. Each category warrants different handling controls, access policies, and retention schedules. Breach notification requirements vary by state but generally require timely disclosure to affected individuals. Institutions that have already classified their data, documented their data flows, and established an incident response protocol can execute breach notification efficiently. Those that haven't will spend the first seventy-two hours of a crisis just trying to figure out what was exposed.
