# Strategic Technology Integration in Education

Most educational institutions accumulate technology the way old houses accumulate wiring -- one system at a time, each solving an immediate problem, none of them talking to each other. The result is a tangle of siloed platforms: an SIS that doesn't feed cleanly into the LMS, a CRM bolted onto an admissions workflow that predates it by a decade, and shadow IT solutions that departments adopted because central IT couldn't move fast enough. Strategic technology integration starts by acknowledging this reality rather than pretending you can rip and replace your way to coherence.

Reference architectures like TOGAF offer a useful starting framework, but they need significant adaptation for higher education. TOGAF assumes a level of centralized authority over technology decisions that most universities simply don't have -- faculty governance, decentralized budgets, and departmental autonomy all work against top-down architecture mandates. What does translate well is the emphasis on business architecture as the foundation. In education, that means mapping technology investments directly to institutional mission: student success, research output, community engagement, operational efficiency. When a provost can see that a proposed integration between the early-alert system and the advising platform directly supports the retention strategy, the conversation shifts from "IT wants more money" to "this investment advances our goals."

Building a technology roadmap that leadership will actually follow requires brutal prioritization and honest timelines. A useful roadmap identifies three to five integration priorities per year, specifies what has to happen sequentially versus in parallel, and names the governance body responsible for each decision. It should also include explicit off-ramps -- points where leadership can reassess based on what's been learned. The institutions that succeed at strategic integration treat it as an ongoing discipline, not a one-time planning exercise. They embed architecture review into procurement, project approval, and annual budgeting so that every new system is evaluated against the integration strategy before a contract is signed.
