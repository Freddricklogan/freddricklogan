# Blockchain Governance in Education

Blockchain technology offers education a fundamentally different model for managing credentials, academic records, and institutional trust -- but it also introduces governance challenges that traditional systems don't have. When you put a credential on a blockchain, you're making a decision about permanence, access, and authority that is much harder to reverse than updating a database record. Governance structures need to be in place before the first transaction is written, not retrofitted after deployment.

Credential verification is the most mature use case for blockchain in education. Instead of employers calling a registrar's office to verify a degree, a blockchain-based credential can be independently verified by anyone with the public key -- instantly, without institutional involvement. This reduces administrative burden and eliminates the risk of fraudulent credentials. But it raises governance questions: Who issues the credential? Who can revoke it? What happens when an institution closes? How do you handle a credential that was issued in error? These aren't technical questions; they're policy questions that require clear governance frameworks, dispute resolution processes, and inter-institutional agreements.

Interoperability between institutions is perhaps the biggest unsolved challenge. For blockchain credentials to deliver on their promise, they need to be recognizable and verifiable across institutional boundaries. This requires shared standards for credential schemas, consensus on which blockchain networks to use, and agreements about how nodes are operated and by whom. Efforts like the W3C Verifiable Credentials standard and initiatives from IMS Global (now 1EdTech) are moving in the right direction, but adoption is uneven and the governance structures for multi-institutional blockchain networks are still being defined.

The decentralization question is where education's governance needs diverge most from the broader blockchain community. Fully decentralized, permissionless blockchains don't fit educational use cases well -- institutions need the ability to correct errors, revoke credentials, and maintain regulatory compliance. Permissioned blockchains, where a consortium of institutions controls network participation, are a better fit. But consortium governance brings its own complexities: voting rights, cost sharing, technical standards, and what happens when a member institution disagrees with a network decision. Institutions considering blockchain should invest as much in governance design as in technical implementation.
