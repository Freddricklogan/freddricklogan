# AI Ethics for Educational Technology

The adoption of AI in education is outpacing the development of ethical frameworks to govern it. Institutions are deploying AI-powered tools for admissions screening, early alert systems, adaptive learning platforms, automated grading, and student retention prediction -- often without fully understanding how these systems make decisions or what biases they may encode. The urgency isn't hypothetical: an algorithm that flags at-risk students based on historical data can perpetuate existing inequities if the training data reflects systemic disparities in outcomes across race, income, or first-generation status.

Algorithmic bias in student assessment is the most immediate ethical concern. When a predictive model trained on five years of institutional data recommends which students should receive intervention resources, it necessarily reflects the patterns of the past -- including patterns of unequal access, implicit bias in grading, and structural barriers that affected some populations more than others. Without explicit bias auditing, these tools can systematically under-serve the students who need support most. Institutions should require vendors to provide documentation of their model training data, validation methodology, and bias testing results before any tool is deployed in a student-facing context.

Transparency and consent form the second pillar. Students have a right to know when AI is being used to make decisions about their education -- whether that's course recommendations, financial aid determinations, or intervention targeting. This doesn't mean publishing model weights; it means providing clear, accessible explanations of what the system does, what data it uses, and how students can contest or opt out of automated decisions. The EU's approach to AI transparency offers useful principles even for US institutions not subject to those regulations.

Human oversight must remain non-negotiable. AI should augment human judgment, not replace it. An early alert system can flag a student who may be struggling, but the decision to intervene -- and how -- should involve an advisor who understands context that no model can capture. Institutions should establish AI review committees, modeled on IRBs, that evaluate proposed AI deployments for ethical implications before they go live. These committees need to include not just technologists and administrators, but faculty, students, and community representatives who can speak to the lived impact of automated decision-making.
